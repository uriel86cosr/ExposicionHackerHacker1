Crackers

is used to describe an individual who engages in breaching computer systems or computer networks with malicious fines. Unlike ethical hackers, crackers use their skills to commit computer crimes, such as stealing information, installing malware, or destroying systems. It is important to note that the term "cracker" is less common than the term "hacker", and is used primarily to distinguish individuals who use their computer skills for criminal purposes from those who do so for ethical or constructive purposes. Most people involved in computer security prefer to use the term "hacker" to refer to all people with advanced computer skills, regardless of whether their intentions are ethical or not.

difference

Therefore, unlike hackers, their goals are almost always malicious. Crackers use their technical knowledge exclusively to invade systems, crack keys and passwords, steal data and other illicit digital uses. Its modus operandi is based on exploiting the vulnerabilities of computer programs and systems, stealing private information to make personal and malicious use of it.

As for their objectives, they are usually related to profit, although sometimes they act for the simple challenge of breaking these security barriers.

Most famous Cracker cases

John Thomas Draper, also known as "Captain Crunch", is a famous American phreaker and hacker known for discovering a vulnerability in telephone systems from the 1970s. His technique involved emitting a series of specific tones through a telephone, which allowed him to make long distance calls for free. Draper was arrested on charges related to tampering with telephone systems in 1971 and sentenced to five years in prison, but he became a legendary figure in the history of computer security.

Charlie Miller is an American exploit developer and computer security expert who became known for his work investigating vulnerabilities in mobile devices and computers. He discovered several major vulnerabilities in Apple's iOS operating system, for which he was awarded a Pwnie Award in 2008 and 2009. In 2011, he was kicked out of the Black Hat computer security conference after hacking into a car to demonstrate security vulnerabilities. security. He currently works on autonomous vehicle safety at Uber.

Albert González Albert González is one of the most famous crackers in history, known for leading a gang of computer criminals who stole millions of credit cards from various American retail stores. González specialized in SQL injection attacks, allowing him to access credit card databases stored on store servers such as Target and TJX Companies. In 2009, González was captured after a joint investigation by the United States Secret Service and Miami police. He was sentenced to 20 years in prison for conspiracy to commit electronic fraud and other crimes related to hacking.

Max Ray Butler Max Ray Butler, also known as "Max Vision", is a cracker and former computer security analyst who was convicted of stealing information from thousands of credit cards and conducting online fraud. Butler is especially known for developing and leading a gang of crackers who specialized in stealing credit card information through phishing attacks. Butler founded the computer security company iSight Partners in 2010, which was purchased by FireEye in 2016. However, in 2011, Butler was convicted of several hacking-related crimes and sentenced to 13 years in prison. Butler is also known for his role in creating the CardersMarket website, which specialized in selling stolen card information

Kane Gamble, who used to be known online as "Cracker," was the most prominent member of the hacking group "Crackers with Attitude" or CWA. In late 2016, Gamble wreaked havoc by hacking into the personal accounts of several U.S. government officials, Including CIA Director John Brennan's email account and Director of National Intelligence James Clapper's home internet account, as well as several U.S. government portals and tools. At the time, Gamble was only 15 years old. He was eventually arrested and sentenced to two years in prison. Even after his release, Gamble was not allowed to use a computer and was banned from using anything that connected to the Internet for a year.
